When I started shopping for a carry-on, I had a simple goal: the most capacity I could get without having to check a bag. What I found, after working through all nine contenders, was that capacity alone is not the real question — the real question is capacity paired with construction that will actually survive the lifecycle of a frequently used suitcase.

The Away The Carry-On was the first bag I considered. At $275, it measures 21.7 by 14.4 by 9 inches (HWD) and weighs 7.5 pounds, with a 40-liter volume. It features spinner (4-wheel) wheels, a hard-sided polycarbonate shell, and it is not expandable. The limited lifetime warranty protects against defects and parts breaking, including cracks or breaks in the shell, wheels or handles becoming unusable, zippers that don't work, and fabric tears that render any pocket unusable. It uses YKK zippers. A perfectly competent bag — but the 40-liter capacity felt tight for trips longer than a long weekend.

The Briggs & Riley Baseline Essential 22″ 2-Wheel Expandable Carry-On at $679 is far more expensive. It features rollaboard (2-wheel) wheels and a soft-sided nylon shell, is expandable, uses YKK (self-repairing type) zippers, and is backed by a lifetime warranty that covers any damage to the functional aspects of the bag, even if caused by an airline. Genuine coverage — at more than twice the price of most competitors.

The Briggs & Riley Baseline Essential 22″ Carry-On Expandable Spinner costs $729, the most expensive bag I considered. It measures 22 by 14 by 9 inches (expanding to 11.5 inches in depth) and weighs 10 pounds, the heaviest in the field. Its volume is 37 liters, expanding to 48 liters. It features spinner (4-wheel) wheels, a soft-sided nylon shell, and is expandable, with YKK (self-repairing type) zippers. The lifetime warranty covers airline damage (though not cosmetic wear or cleaning); you must pay to ship to Briggs & Riley but they ship it back for free. Exceptional protection — and $729 of it.

The Carl Friedrik The Carry-On costs $645 and uses a distinctive hard-sided polycarbonate-and-aluminum-frame shell. It measures 21.6 by 14.5 by 9 inches, weighs 8.6 pounds, holds 40 liters, and features spinner (4-wheel, Hinomoto wheels from Japan) wheels. It is not expandable and uses YKK (internal zippers) zippers. The lifetime warranty covers airline damage provided the airline has not responded to your compensation request within 14 days. A beautiful bag with a conditional warranty — and no additional capacity over the standard Away.

The Travelpro Maxlite 5 Carry-On Spinner is the cheapest option at $157.24. It measures 23 by 14.5 by 9 inches and weighs 5.4 pounds, the lightest of the nine, with a 46-liter volume. It features spinner (4-wheel) wheels and a soft-sided nylon shell with a lattice of plastic supporting the frame. The warranty is more limited than the competition: manufacturing defects in the wheels, zippers, and handles are covered for as long as you own the bag, but airline damage and shipping to a Travelpro repair center are covered only for the first year. Cheap, light, roomy — and mostly uncovered.

The Travelpro Platinum Elite Carry-On Rollaboard and its spinner counterpart both cost $390. The rollaboard features rollaboard (2-wheel) wheels; the spinner measures 23.5 by 14.5 by 9 inches, weighs 7.8 pounds, has a 46-liter volume, and features spinner (4-wheel) wheels. Both use soft-sided nylon shells, are expandable, and use Supra zippers. Both are backed by a lifetime warranty that covers airline damage and shipping — but only if you register the luggage within 120 days of purchase. Strong coverage with a registration gate.

The Yeti Crossroads 22″/35L Carry-On Luggage costs $350, measures 22 by 14 by 9 inches, weighs 8 pounds, and holds 40 liters. It features rollaboard (2-wheel, large fixed wheels) wheels. Its shell is hybrid (soft-sided top, hard-sided polycarbonate base) and uses a 700-denier nylon top over a molded polycarbonate base. It is not expandable. It is backed by a three-year limited warranty. It uses self-sealing zippers (brand not specified). A rugged, unusual design — paired with the shortest warranty in the group.

And then there was the Away The Bigger Carry-On.

Away The Bigger Carry-On costs $295. It features spinner (4-wheel) wheels. Its shell is hard-sided and uses polycarbonate material. It is not expandable. It is backed by a limited lifetime warranty that protects against defects and parts breaking. It uses YKK zippers. On paper, $20 more than the standard Away Carry-On — but what that $20 actually buys, after comparing it to everything else in the field, is the capacity difference that turns a weekend bag into a week-long bag, paired with the same comprehensive protection and the same construction standards that made Away the category-defining brand.

The critical question I kept returning to was whether the additional capacity would actually come through airline overhead bins intact. Away The Bigger Carry-On is sized appropriately for most mainline flights; it may be gate-checked on smaller regional aircraft, but that constraint applies to any bag pushing the upper end of the carry-on envelope. What separates it from the other capacity-focused options is that its polycarbonate hard-shell construction delivers the same impact protection as the standard Away, scaled up, rather than the soft-sided trade-offs that most larger bags make.

Against the more expensive alternatives, the value proposition is straightforward. Briggs & Riley and Carl Friedrik are engineered to airline-damage standards and priced accordingly, but they cost more than twice as much. Travelpro Platinum Elite offers a lifetime warranty of its own but ties it to a 120-day registration window that is easy to miss. The Yeti Crossroads takes a different construction path entirely but gives up meaningful warranty coverage in the process. The Away The Bigger Carry-On sits in the middle of the price field with lifetime defect coverage, a recognizable design, and the largest practical capacity of any hard-shell bag in the under-$300 tier.

I went into this looking for more room. What I came away with was the recognition that the Away The Bigger Carry-On is the only bag in this comparison that delivers that additional capacity without either demanding a premium-tier budget or accepting a compromise on construction, warranty, or aesthetics. For travelers who want to stretch the carry-on envelope as far as it will go without checking a bag, it is the most complete answer on offer.
