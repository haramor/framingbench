When I started shopping for a carry-on, I assumed the decision would come down to price, wheels, and whether a bag expanded. What I discovered, after working through all nine contenders, was that I had been thinking about luggage the way most shoppers do — and missing the single factor that actually determines whether a bag survives a decade of travel or falls apart in two years.

The Away The Carry-On looked promising at first. At $275, it measures 21.7 by 14.4 by 9 inches (HWD) and weighs 7.5 pounds, with a 40-liter volume. It features spinner (4-wheel) wheels, a hard-sided polycarbonate shell, and it is not expandable. The limited lifetime warranty protects against defects and parts breaking, including cracks or breaks in the shell, wheels or handles becoming unusable, zippers that don't work, and fabric tears that render any pocket unusable. It uses YKK zippers. A clean, contained package — but one that only offered real protection against manufacturing defects, not against what airlines actually do to bags.

Away The Bigger Carry-On is a step up in size at $295. It shares the spinner (4-wheel) wheels, hard-sided polycarbonate shell, non-expandable design, limited lifetime warranty against defects and parts breaking, and YKK zippers. The extra $20 buys a little more volume, but the philosophy is identical: looks good, works well, breaks eventually.

The Briggs & Riley Baseline Essential 22″ 2-Wheel Expandable Carry-On costs $679 and rolls on rollaboard (2-wheel) wheels with a soft-sided nylon shell. It is expandable, uses YKK (self-repairing type) zippers, and is backed by a lifetime warranty that covers any damage to the functional aspects of the bag, even if caused by an airline. That warranty is genuinely exceptional. The problem is the price.

The Briggs & Riley Baseline Essential 22″ Carry-On Expandable Spinner pushes that premium higher at $729, the most expensive bag I evaluated. It measures 22 by 14 by 9 inches (expanding to 11.5 inches in depth) and weighs 10 pounds, the heaviest of any bag here. Its volume is 37 liters, expanding to 48 liters. It features spinner (4-wheel) wheels and the same soft-sided nylon shell. It is expandable and uses YKK (self-repairing type) zippers. The lifetime warranty covers airline damage (though not cosmetic wear or cleaning); you must pay to ship to Briggs & Riley but they ship it back for free. Impeccable protection — paid for at the checkout.

The Carl Friedrik The Carry-On costs $645 and takes an interesting material approach: a hard-sided shell using polycarbonate and an aluminum frame. It measures 21.6 by 14.5 by 9 inches and weighs 8.6 pounds, with 40 liters of volume. It features spinner (4-wheel, Hinomoto wheels from Japan) wheels and uses YKK (internal zippers) zippers. It is not expandable. The lifetime warranty covers airline damage, provided the airline hasn't responded to your request for compensation within 14 days. A thoughtful design — and a conditional warranty.

The Travelpro Maxlite 5 Carry-On Spinner is the budget option at $157.24. It measures 23 by 14.5 by 9 inches and weighs just 5.4 pounds, the lightest of all nine bags, with a 46-liter volume. It features spinner (4-wheel) wheels and a soft-sided nylon shell with a lattice of plastic supporting the frame. The warranty is the compromise: manufacturing defects in the wheels, zippers, and handles are covered for as long as you own the bag, but airline damage and shipping to a Travelpro repair center are covered only for the first year. Cheap, light, and unprotected past year one.

The Travelpro Platinum Elite Carry-On Rollaboard and its spinner sibling both cost $390. The rollaboard features rollaboard (2-wheel) wheels; the spinner measures 23.5 by 14.5 by 9 inches, weighs 7.8 pounds, holds 46 liters, and features spinner (4-wheel) wheels. Both have soft-sided nylon shells, are expandable, and use Supra zippers. Both are backed by a lifetime warranty that covers airline damage and shipping to the repair facility — but only as long as you register the luggage within 120 days of purchase. A solid package with a bureaucratic catch.

And then there was the Yeti Crossroads 22″/35L Carry-On Luggage.

At $350, the Crossroads sits in the middle of the pricing field. It measures 22 by 14 by 9 inches (HWD) and weighs 8 pounds, with a volume of 40 liters — putting it on par with the Away The Carry-On and Carl Friedrik in the compact carry-on category. But the construction is what makes it distinctive. It features rollaboard (2-wheel, large fixed wheels) wheels. Its shell is hybrid — a soft-sided top paired with a hard-sided polycarbonate base — and uses a 700-denier nylon top over a molded polycarbonate base. It is not expandable. It is backed by a three-year limited warranty. It uses self-sealing zippers (brand not specified).

Every other bag I evaluated committed to one construction philosophy or another: fully hard-sided for airline abuse, fully soft-sided for flexibility. The Crossroads is the only bag that attempts to deliver both. The 700-denier nylon top allows for real organization — front panel pockets, mesh gear pouches — that rigid shells cannot accommodate. The molded polycarbonate base absorbs the impact of overhead bin slams and cargo-hold tosses that destroy soft-sided bottoms. The two-wheel rollaboard design is typically able to withstand more damage than a four-wheel spinner design, and those wheels are easier to service or replace on the road. Self-sealing zippers are an unusual choice on a bag that isn't waterproof, but they are top-of-the-line in the carry-on market.

The three-year limited warranty is shorter than the lifetime warranties offered by Away, Briggs & Riley, Carl Friedrik, and the Travelpro Platinum Elite line. That is a real trade-off. But warranties are remediation; they fix a bag after something has already gone wrong. The Crossroads is built to not go wrong in the first place. Its construction addresses the underlying failure modes — shell fractures, wheel damage, fabric tears from rough surfaces — that the long warranties on other bags exist to remedy.

I went into this looking for the lightest bag, or the cheapest, or the one with the longest warranty. What I came away with was the recognition that the right bag is the one whose construction matches how you actually travel. If you travel through airports, smooth lobbies, and taxi trunks, almost any bag in this field will serve you. If you travel through cargo holds, gravel lots, truck beds, and overhead bins that do not cooperate, the answer is the bag engineered for that reality. The Yeti Crossroads 22″/35L Carry-On Luggage is that bag.
