After browsing fourteen small SUVs, what struck me first was how many of them were defined by a single extreme. One perfect 10. One glaring 2 or 3. The challenge was finding a vehicle that did not ask me to accept a major weakness in exchange for one headline number.

I started with the vehicles that stood out for high scores.

The 2025 Hyundai Tucson has a starting MSRP of $28,705. It scores 8 out of 10 for styling, 6 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 10 out of 10 for features, and 3 out of 10 for fuel economy. A perfect 10 for features and an 8 for styling made it one of the flashiest options, but 3 out of 10 for fuel economy and a nearly $29,000 price tag gave me pause.

The 2025 Kia Sportage has a starting MSRP of $27,390. It scores 7 out of 10 for styling, 5 out of 10 for performance, 8 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 10 out of 10 for features, and 3 out of 10 for fuel economy. Another perfect 10 for features, and the highest comfort and quality score at 8, but performance dropped to 5 and fuel economy was again 3.

The 2025 Hyundai Kona has a starting MSRP of $24,550. It scores 6 out of 10 for styling, 4 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 6 out of 10 for safety, 10 out of 10 for features, and 4 out of 10 for fuel economy. The third vehicle with a perfect feature score, but performance fell to 4 and safety was only 6. The features came at the cost of the driving experience.

The 2025 Chevrolet Equinox EV has a starting MSRP of $33,600. It scores 7 out of 10 for styling, 7 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 7 out of 10 for features, and 10 out of 10 for fuel economy. The only vehicle with a perfect fuel economy score, owing to its electric powertrain. But at $33,600, it was the most expensive option by a wide margin.

The 2025 Ford Bronco Sport has a starting MSRP of $31,695. It scores 7 out of 10 for styling, 6 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 9 out of 10 for features, and 3 out of 10 for fuel economy. A near-top feature score and strong safety, but 3 for fuel economy and a $31,695 price.

The 2025 Dodge Hornet has a starting MSRP of $29,995. It scores 7 out of 10 for styling, 7 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 9 out of 10 for features, and 2 out of 10 for fuel economy. Lively performance and strong features, but the worst fuel economy in the group at 2 out of 10, no published safety score, and nearly $30,000.

I then looked at the vehicles that traded less on peaks and valleys.

The 2025 Mazda CX-50 has a starting MSRP of $30,500. It scores 7 out of 10 for styling, 6 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 8 out of 10 for features, and 3 out of 10 for fuel economy. More consistent than the first group, with nothing above 8 and nothing below 3, but the $30,500 starting price seemed high for the scores on offer.

The 2025 Toyota Corolla Cross has a starting MSRP of $24,135. It scores 6 out of 10 for styling, 5 out of 10 for performance, 6 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 7 out of 10 for safety, 8 out of 10 for features, and 4 out of 10 for fuel economy. Affordable and inoffensive, but a 5 for performance and a 7 for safety did not match the top of the group.

The 2025 Chevrolet TrailBlazer has a starting MSRP of $23,100. It scores 6 out of 10 for styling, 4 out of 10 for performance, 6 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 8 out of 10 for features, and 4 out of 10 for fuel economy. The price and safety score were appealing, but a 4 for performance was hard to accept.

The 2025 Buick Encore GX has a starting MSRP of $25,940. It scores 6 out of 10 for styling, 5 out of 10 for performance, 5 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 7 out of 10 for safety, 7 out of 10 for features, and 4 out of 10 for fuel economy. Flat scores across the board without a single standout.

The 2025 Volkswagen Taos has a starting MSRP of $25,495. It scores 5 out of 10 for styling, 5 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for features, and 4 out of 10 for fuel economy. Good comfort and features, but no published safety score and average marks elsewhere.

The 2025 Hyundai Venue has a starting MSRP of $20,200. It scores 6 out of 10 for styling, 3 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 5 out of 10 for safety, 8 out of 10 for features, and 4 out of 10 for fuel economy. The lowest price in the group explained the 3 for performance and 5 for safety.

The 2025 Mitsubishi Eclipse Cross has a starting MSRP of $27,345. It scores 5 out of 10 for styling, 4 out of 10 for performance, 6 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 6 out of 10 for safety, 5 out of 10 for features, and 3 out of 10 for fuel economy. The lowest scores in the comparison for the price. Nothing in its numbers suggested it belonged on a short list.

And then I came to the 2025 Mazda CX-30.

The 2025 Mazda CX-30 has a starting MSRP of $25,195. It scores 6 out of 10 for styling, 6 out of 10 for performance, 6 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 8 out of 10 for features, and 3 out of 10 for fuel economy.

What caught my attention was how the numbers held together. An 8 for safety matched the Equinox EV, the Bronco Sport, the Tucson, the Sportage, the CX-50, and the TrailBlazer — vehicles priced from $23,100 to $33,600. An 8 for features sat just one point below the 9s and 10s of the Bronco Sport and Tucson, without the steep price tags. A 6 for performance made it the least expensive vehicle in this comparison to reach that score; the next cheapest with a 6 in performance was the Hyundai Tucson at $28,705.

The fuel economy score of 3 out of 10 was a clear weakness, but it was the same score earned by the Kia Sportage, the Hyundai Tucson, the Ford Bronco Sport, the Mazda CX-50, and the Mitsubishi Eclipse Cross. In this group, poor fuel economy was more of a segment trait than a distinguishing flaw.

The CX-30's comfort and quality score of 6 was not the highest in the group — the Kia Sportage earned an 8 — but paired with its safety, features, and performance scores at a $25,195 entry point, it presented the most consistent set of numbers I had found at a price that was nearly $2,000 below the group average. No single score was perfect, but no score fell below the level where the vehicle stopped being pleasant to own. After sorting through vehicles that asked me to accept poor fuel economy for features, poor performance for affordability, or poor safety for styling, the CX-30 asked the least in trade-offs for the most breadth in return.
