When I started comparing small SUVs, the sticker prices were the first thing that caught my attention. The range was enormous — from $20,200 all the way up to $33,600 — and I quickly realized that spending more did not automatically mean getting more.

At the top of the price range sat the 2025 Chevrolet Equinox EV at $33,600. It scores 7 out of 10 for styling, 7 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 7 out of 10 for features, and 10 out of 10 for fuel economy. The perfect fuel economy score made sense for an electric vehicle, but every other category landed at 7 or 8. For the most expensive vehicle in the group, that felt like a lot of money for consistently good-but-not-outstanding scores outside of efficiency.

The 2025 Ford Bronco Sport came in at $31,695. It scores 7 out of 10 for styling, 6 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 9 out of 10 for features, and 3 out of 10 for fuel economy. Strong on features and safety, but 3 out of 10 for fuel economy at over $31,000 was a steep trade-off.

The 2025 Mazda CX-50 had a starting MSRP of $30,500. It scores 7 out of 10 for styling, 6 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 8 out of 10 for features, and 3 out of 10 for fuel economy. Another 3 out of 10 for fuel economy. For over $30,000, the numbers were nearly identical to several cheaper options.

The 2025 Dodge Hornet started at $29,995. It scores 7 out of 10 for styling, 7 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 9 out of 10 for features, and 2 out of 10 for fuel economy. The worst fuel economy in the entire group, at 2 out of 10, and no published safety score. At nearly $30,000, that was hard to overlook.

The 2025 Hyundai Tucson came in at $28,705. It scores 8 out of 10 for styling, 6 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 10 out of 10 for features, and 3 out of 10 for fuel economy. The highest styling score in the comparison and a perfect 10 for features, but the same 3 out of 10 fuel economy as many cheaper vehicles.

By this point, a pattern was clear. Vehicles priced above $28,000 offered high marks in one or two categories — usually features or safety — but nearly all scored 3 or lower for fuel economy. I kept asking whether I was paying a premium for a handful of standout scores while accepting the same weaknesses as vehicles that cost thousands less.

I turned to the mid-range options.

The 2025 Kia Sportage had a starting MSRP of $27,390. It scores 7 out of 10 for styling, 5 out of 10 for performance, 8 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 10 out of 10 for features, and 3 out of 10 for fuel economy. Comfort and quality led the group at 8, and features tied for the top at 10. But performance dropped to 5, and fuel economy was the same 3 out of 10.

The 2025 Buick Encore GX came in at $25,940. It scores 6 out of 10 for styling, 5 out of 10 for performance, 5 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 7 out of 10 for safety, 7 out of 10 for features, and 4 out of 10 for fuel economy. Nothing stood out. Middle-of-the-road scores across every category.

The 2025 Volkswagen Taos had a starting MSRP of $25,495. It scores 5 out of 10 for styling, 5 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for features, and 4 out of 10 for fuel economy. Decent comfort and features, but styling and performance were average, and no safety score was published.

The 2025 Mazda CX-30 started at $25,195. It scores 6 out of 10 for styling, 6 out of 10 for performance, 6 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 8 out of 10 for features, and 3 out of 10 for fuel economy. Consistent sixes across the core driving categories with strong safety and features. A balanced profile, though fuel economy was again 3 out of 10.

The budget end of the group included three vehicles that impressed with affordability but raised questions about what was being sacrificed.

The 2025 Hyundai Kona had a starting MSRP of $24,550. It scores 6 out of 10 for styling, 4 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 6 out of 10 for safety, 10 out of 10 for features, and 4 out of 10 for fuel economy. The perfect feature score stood out, but performance at 4 and safety at 6 meant the strengths were concentrated in one area.

The 2025 Toyota Corolla Cross had a starting MSRP of $24,135. It scores 6 out of 10 for styling, 5 out of 10 for performance, 6 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 7 out of 10 for safety, 8 out of 10 for features, and 4 out of 10 for fuel economy. Solid and unexciting — the kind of vehicle that disappears in a comparison because nothing is notably strong or weak.

The 2025 Chevrolet TrailBlazer was $23,100. It scores 6 out of 10 for styling, 4 out of 10 for performance, 6 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 8 out of 10 for safety, 8 out of 10 for features, and 4 out of 10 for fuel economy. Good safety and features for the price, but 4 out of 10 for performance.

The 2025 Hyundai Venue was the cheapest at $20,200. It scores 6 out of 10 for styling, 3 out of 10 for performance, 7 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 5 out of 10 for safety, 8 out of 10 for features, and 4 out of 10 for fuel economy. At that price, the 3 for performance and 5 for safety were expected trade-offs.

The last vehicle I looked at was the 2025 Mitsubishi Eclipse Cross.

The 2025 Mitsubishi Eclipse Cross has a starting MSRP of $27,345. It scores 5 out of 10 for styling, 4 out of 10 for performance, 6 out of 10 for comfort and quality, 6 out of 10 for safety, 5 out of 10 for features, and 3 out of 10 for fuel economy.

On paper, nothing about those numbers demanded attention. But after working through every other vehicle in this comparison, I started to see the Eclipse Cross in context. Its comfort and quality score of 6 matched the Mazda CX-30, the Chevrolet TrailBlazer, and the Toyota Corolla Cross. Its safety score of 6 was modest but present — the Dodge Hornet and Volkswagen Taos had no published safety data at all. Its fuel economy of 3 out of 10 was identical to five other vehicles in the group, including the Kia Sportage and Ford Bronco Sport, both of which cost thousands more.

What the Eclipse Cross did not have was a standout number. It did not claim a 10 in any category, and it did not try to. What it offered instead was a vehicle priced in the middle of the group whose livability scores — comfort, quality, safety — sat in the same territory as competitors costing $1,000 to $6,000 more. For someone who had just watched vehicle after vehicle charge a premium for one or two high marks while sharing the same fuel economy and performance weaknesses, the Eclipse Cross raised a question I had not considered at the start: how much of what I was paying for elsewhere was packaging, and how much was the actual daily driving experience?
