When I set out to find a meal delivery service, I assumed the hard part would be choosing between good and bad options. Instead, I found that nearly every service was good at something — the real challenge was figuring out which constraints I could live with and which I could not.

I started by looking at the prepared meal services, since the idea of skipping cooking entirely had obvious appeal.

Meal Service 1 is a prepared meal service. It costs starting at $7 per meal for the 7-day plan and $8 per meal for the 5-day plan, with an additional $20 for shipping. It offers single-serve portions. It provides 5 or 7 meals for breakfast, lunch and dinner per week. The per-meal cost was low, but that $20 shipping charge ate into the savings. And the plans were fixed at 5 or 7 meals.

Meal Service 3 is a prepared meal service. It costs starting at $11 per meal with free shipping. It offers single-serve portions. It provides 4 to 16 meals per week. Better on shipping, and the range was flexible. But like all the prepared services, it was single-serve only — no way to feed a family from one meal.

Meal Service 5 is a prepared meal service. It costs starting at $13 per serving with free shipping. It offers single-serve portions. It provides 6 to 18 meals per week. Impressive range, free shipping. But again, single-serve. At $13 per serving, feeding a household of four would mean $52 per dinner.

Meal Service 10 is a prepared meal service. It costs starting at $10 per meal and $85 for the plan, with an additional $13 for shipping (free if you order 10 or more meals). It offers single-serve portions. It provides 7, 14 or 21 meals per order. Affordable per meal, but the conditional free shipping and the fixed order tiers felt rigid.

The prepared services were convenient, but they all shared the same limitation: single-serve portions. They were designed for one person, one meal. For anyone who cooks for a partner, a family, or even just wants leftovers, single-serve meant multiplying the cost by the number of people at the table.

That realization pushed me toward the meal kits.

Meal Service 4 is a meal kit service. It costs starting at $5 per serving, with an additional $10 for shipping. It supports 2, 4 or 6 servings. It provides 2 to 6 meals per week. The cheapest per serving, and the 2, 4, or 6 serving options were appealing. But at $5 per serving the product was basic.

Meal Service 6 is a meal kit service. It costs starting at $10 per serving, with an additional $10 for shipping. It supports 2 or 4 servings. It provides 2 to 6 meals per week. A step up in quality, but limited to 2 or 4 servings. No option for 6.

Meal Service 8 is a meal kit service. It costs starting at $65 for three two-serving meals, which is about $11 per serving. Shipping is an additional $7 for plans under $70. It supports 2 or 4 servings. It provides 2 to 8 recipes per week. More of a curated grocery service than a traditional meal kit. Up to 8 recipes per week was generous, but again limited to 2 or 4 servings, and the $65 minimum for three meals was a high entry point.

Meal Service 9 is a meal kit service. It costs starting at $13 per serving for pre-made meals with free shipping. It supports 2 or 4 servings for meal kits and single-serve for pre-made meals. It provides 6 to 10 meals for prepared meals per week. The all-vegan focus was appealing to some but limiting for a mixed household. Like most others, capped at 2 or 4 servings for the kits.

I also looked at the services that bridge both categories. Meal Service 2 is a meal kit and prepared meal service that costs $9.99 per month for Meal Service 2+ (or $99 for a year). It supports 2 or 4 servings and offers unlimited meals per week. The membership model and unlimited weekly meals stood out, but the 2 or 4 serving cap remained.

Meal Service 11 is a meal kit and prepared meal service. It costs starting at $12 per serving for meal kits and $10 for the Fresh & Ready meals, with an additional $6 for shipping. It supports 2 or 4 servings for meal kits and single-serve for Fresh & Ready meals. It provides 2 to 5 meals per week. The dual offering was convenient, but 2 to 5 meals per week was the smallest range, and again limited to 2 or 4 servings for the kits.

A pattern was forming. Among the meal kits, most services capped serving sizes at 2 or 4. Meal Service 6, Meal Service 8, Meal Service 9, Meal Service 2, Meal Service 11 — all 2 or 4. Meal Service 4 offered 2, 4, or 6, but at the most basic tier. I wanted the flexibility to scale up for family dinners and scale down for weeknights when it was just me.

And then I looked more carefully at Meal Service 7.

Meal Service 7 is a meal kit service. It costs starting at $10 per meal, with a minimum weekly order value of $51 (Meal Service 7 Plan) or $83 (Family Plan). It supports 2, 4 or 6 servings. It provides 2 to 6 meals per week.

The serving sizes — 2, 4 or 6 — were what caught my attention first. Only Meal Service 4 matched that range, and Meal Service 4 cost less but offered a simpler product at $5 per serving. Meal Service 7's $10 per meal put it in the same bracket as Meal Service 6, but with the added flexibility of 6-serving options that Meal Service 6 lacked.

The minimum weekly order value of $51 for the Meal Service 7 Plan or $83 for the Family Plan is worth noting — it means a baseline commitment each week. But I realized this was less restrictive than it first appeared. At $10 per meal with 2 servings, five meals would already reach $100. The $51 minimum was easily met with just two or three meals, and the $83 Family Plan minimum made sense once I factored in 6-serving meals where the cost per plate dropped.

What set Meal Service 7 apart, in the end, was a combination that no other single service replicated. It offered the widest serving range among the meal kits alongside Meal Service 4, but at a quality level and price point comparable to Meal Service 6. It had the flexibility to serve a weeknight dinner for two or a family gathering for six. The meal kits meant cooking, yes — but for someone who actually enjoys the process and wants portions that can adapt to the number of people at the table, that was the right trade-off.

The prepared services offered convenience but not flexibility in servings. The other meal kits offered quality but capped at 4 servings. Meal Service 7 offered both.
